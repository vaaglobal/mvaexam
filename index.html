About the patient data
Read this carefully before starting any project
What is this data? You have been provided with a complete, hypothetical patient record for a patient named Mr. Daniel James Thompson Jr. This is a fictional record created for training purposes only — it is not a real person. The record contains everything a medical office would collect when a new patient registers: their personal details, contact information, employer, insurance, and communication preferences.
Why does it exist? In a real medical practice, you will receive patient information like this every day. As a Medical VA, your job is to take that raw information and accurately enter it into various systems — intake forms, databases, and EMR software. Making mistakes here causes real problems: wrong insurance details can lead to rejected claims, wrong contact info means the patient never gets their appointment reminder, and missing emergency contacts can be dangerous. Accuracy is everything.
How should you use the data in these projects? Each project tells you exactly which fields to use and shows you the exact value to type in. Look for the green pills — those are values from the patient's personal and primary data. Amber pills are secondary insurance (Aetna/Sarah Thompson). Blue pills are tertiary insurance (Medicaid) and EMR steps. Your job is to take those values and enter them exactly as shown into the correct place in the form, table, or system the project asks you to use. Do not guess, do not make up information, and do not skip fields. If a field says N/A, enter N/A exactly as shown.
The record has 9 sections: Who (personal identity), Contact (address and phones), Choices (provider and communication preferences), Employer, Stats (demographics and income), Related/Guardian (spouse details), and three insurance layers — Primary (Blue Cross Blue Shield), Secondary (Aetna), and Tertiary (Medicaid). You will use all 9 sections across these projects.

1
Appointment scheduling script
Script writing / Communication

What you are doing: You are writing a script — like a word-for-word conversation — that a Medical VA would use when calling a patient to schedule their appointment. Think of it like a play script: you write both what you (the VA) say and what prompts you use to gather information from the patient.

Why this matters: In a real clinic, the first time a patient hears from you is often over the phone. If you sound unprepared, forget to ask for their insurance, or fail to mention the co-pay, it creates a bad experience and causes problems on the day of the visit. A well-written script ensures every VA says the right things every time, regardless of how nervous they feel.

What to produce: Write a script that starts from the greeting ("Good morning, may I speak with Mr. Thompson?") and ends with a confirmation. Include a follow-up confirmation message — either an SMS or email — using the patient's actual contact details from the record below.

Airtable patient database — build all 9 tables
Airtable (free account required)

What you are doing: You are building a digital patient database from scratch using Airtable — a free, beginner-friendly tool that works like a spreadsheet but is far more powerful. You will create 9 separate tables, one for each section of the patient record, and enter Daniel Thompson's data across all of them.

Why this matters: In real medical offices, patient data is stored across multiple systems and tables — demographics in one place, insurance in another, employer details in yet another. Learning to organise data this way trains you to think like a records management professional. Airtable also allows you to link tables together, which is exactly how real database systems (like Epic and Athena) work under the hood.

How to get started: Go to airtable.com, create a free account, and create a new base (Airtable's word for a database) called Patient Records. Inside that base, create the 9 tables listed below. For each table, create the field names listed in the left column, then enter the patient data shown in the right column. Once all data is entered, use Airtable's Link to another record field type to connect the Contact, Choices, Insurance, and other tables back to the Patient Information table.

EMR practical — register a patient in OpenEMR
OpenEMR demo (live system)

What you are doing: You are logging into a real, live Electronic Medical Records (EMR) system called OpenEMR and registering Daniel Thompson as a brand new patient. This means you will navigate the actual software that clinics use, find the correct screens, and enter all his information — demographics, preferences, and all three insurance layers — exactly as a working Medical VA would on their first day at a job.

Why this matters: An EMR system is the central nervous system of any medical practice. Every appointment, every prescription, every insurance claim, and every patient communication flows through it. As a Medical VA, you will spend a large portion of your working day inside an EMR. Practising in a demo environment is the safest and best way to get comfortable before going live with real patients. Mistakes in a demo cost nothing. Mistakes in a real system can affect patient care and clinic revenue.

How the demo works: OpenEMR provides a free, publicly accessible demo version of their software. You do not need to install anything — just click the link below, pick any available demo instance, and log in with the credentials shown on that page. The system resets periodically, so your entries may not be saved forever, but that is fine for this exercise. Your deliverable is a screenshot taken immediately after saving.
